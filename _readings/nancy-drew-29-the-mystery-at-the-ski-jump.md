## Hook & thesis
In *The Mystery at the Ski Jump*, Carolyn Keene crafts a quintessential Nancy Drew adventure that blends suspense with the thrill of competition. **Verdict:** This book excels at intertwining friendship with mystery, making it a standout in the series that resonates with readers seeking both intrigue and camaraderie.

## The contextual pivot
As part of the beloved Nancy Drew Mystery Stories, this installment sits comfortably within the middle-grade mystery genre, appealing to both loyal fans and new readers alike. Keene's ability to mix light-hearted adventure with serious themes of competition and trust allows this book to bridge niche tropes, making it accessible to a broader audience. Compared to other titles in the series, it maintains the signature elements of mystery while introducing younger readers to a more competitive backdrop, reminiscent of other light mysteries like *The Hardy Boys*.

## Deep-dive critique
The novel's structure effectively uses a tight third-person perspective, allowing readers to experience the unfolding mystery alongside Nancy while keeping the tension high. The pacing is brisk, particularly during the ski jump scenes, which highlight both the physical stakes and personal dynamics between characters. Keene’s dialogue is sharp and reveals character depth, particularly in Nancy’s interactions with her friends, which enriches the emotional stakes. However, some descriptions of the ski landscape could benefit from deeper world-building to enhance the setting's immersive quality. The ending, wherein all threads are tied together, feels earned, providing a satisfying resolution to both the mystery and the friendships tested throughout.

## Discussion launchpad
While *The Mystery at the Ski Jump* is engaging, it raises questions about the balance between competition and friendship in a youth's life. The book successfully captures the excitement of a mystery but also nudges readers to reflect on their own values. 
- **Questions for the room:**  
  - How do competition and friendship coexist in the lives of young teens?  
  - Does the mystery genre offer a safe space for exploring darker themes?  
  - In what ways do Nancy’s friendships enhance or hinder her investigative skills?  
  - How does Keene's portrayal of female friendships compare to other books you’ve read?  
  - What elements of this mystery resonate with your own experiences?
