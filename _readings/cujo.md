## Hook & thesis
In Stephen King's *Cujo*, the seemingly straightforward tale of a rabid dog becomes a deep exploration of human vulnerability and the fragility of familial bonds. **Verdict:** This novel masterfully juxtaposes the mundane with the horrific, revealing how ordinary lives can unravel in terrifying ways.

## The contextual pivot
*Cujo* occupies a defining space in the dark thriller genre, where horror transcends mere scares to probe the human psyche's deepest fears. King, known for his ability to blend the supernatural with the everyday, creates a narrative that takes the reader into the heart of small-town America, where the invisible audience comprises those seeking more than just a chilling tale; they crave an examination of dread that feels all too real. Compared to other works like Shirley Jackson's *The Haunting of Hill House*, which similarly interrogates psychological terror, *Cujo* leverages its animal antagonist as an embodiment of chaos in a world that is already on the brink.

## Deep-dive critique
King's craft shines particularly in his pacing and character development. The structure unfolds through alternating perspectives, offering insight into the lives of the Trenton family and the dog, Cujo. This duality creates a tension that escalates, enhancing the reader's investment in both human and canine perspectives. The timeline is linear yet punctuated by flashbacks that flesh out the characters' pasts; however, at times it feels as if the pacing drags in places where the buildup could be tighter, particularly in the midsection where human conflicts overshadow the impending crisis.

Dialogue in *Cujo* is another area where King excels, capturing the nuances of small-town life and the complexity of relationships in moments of crisis. The emotional weight of the characters' interactions adds depth, particularly in the strained dynamic between Vic and Wendy Trenton, which mirrors the larger theme of parental protection against insurmountable odds. However, the graphic scenes, particularly those depicting Cujo's violent breakdown, may alienate some readers who seek subtler horror, diluting the psychological tension that defines much of the narrative.

The ending, while shocking and evocative, raises questions about its resolution — is it a catharsis or a critique of the inevitability of fate? King’s choice to leave certain character arcs unresolved could either be seen as an artistic strength or a narrative flaw, depending on the reader's perspective.

## Discussion launchpad
*Cujo* invites polarizing interpretations, especially regarding its thematic exploration of fear and family. How effectively does King balance horror with human emotion, and at what cost?

**Questions for the room:**  
- Does the graphic nature of Cujo’s transformation detract from the psychological themes?  
- How do the Trenton family's struggles reflect broader societal issues of the 1980s?  
- Is the ending of *Cujo* satisfying, or does it leave too many threads unresolved?  
- In what ways does *Cujo* challenge the reader's expectations of a traditional horror story?  
- How does King’s portrayal of animal violence serve the larger narrative?
